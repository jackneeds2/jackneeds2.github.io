The Boise River is a tributary of the Snake River, approximately 75 miles (121 km) long. It drains a rugged portion of the Sawtooth Range in southwestern Idaho northeast of Boise. The Boise River rises in three separate forks in the Sawtooth Range at elevations exceeding 10,000 feet (3048 m), and is formed by the confluence of its North and Middle forks. It has a century-old Boise River Diversion Dam which puts water in the concrete New York Canal, which terminates at Lake Lowell (a.k.a. Deer Flat Reservoir) southwest of Nampa in Canyon County. As the river passes through downtown Boise, it is lined by an extensive recreational greenbelt. The Boise River then flows west across the western end of the Snake River Plain, it becomes a braided stream with a wide floodplain as it crosses northern Canyon County to the Snake River. The river is a popular destination for floating, specifically on the urban tree-lined run through Boise during hot, dry summer afternoons. Tubers and floaters put in at Barber Park and end up at Ann Morrison Park, between major irrigation diversion dams.
